Redesign Prototypes Page

As previously mentioned, the prototype has gone through numerous prototyping and testing phases. This was a major part of the formative evaluation process during development to make sure that the usability was fitting to all stakeholders. The stakeholder group that I wanted to focus most on was the novice user group given that I felt this would represent the majority. I wanted to test to see if users who had average online UI backgrounds could navigate a number of different systems that I had set up. Given that this is the first system of its kind that has been applied to this marketplace, I figured I had a lot of flexibility in trying to develop mental models. However, what I ran into the most was the fact that proceduralization does not mean optimization. More specifically, users want to generate results rather than learn a system. For that reason, I wanted to make the system as easy as possible, and I believe I achieved that. In reality, my system is sort of a proxy to the goal of gaining more revenue because it falls under numerous layers of the issue. More specifically, the layers being: I need more revenue, I need more views for more sales, I need to figure out how to get more views, I need keywords to get more views, I need to know what the best keywords are. Therefore, users have a number of problems to conquer and iterate through before arriving at my system.

Prototype One (Low Fidelity)

(Input)

(Output)

For this first prototype, I decided to make a very quick demo of the system. For this, I just wanted to see whether or not the user (in the absence of documentation) could understand how to get a result out of the system. There was no real functionality yet, the system did not actually parse the internet for the keywords. Rather, it just returned to the user a dictionary of phrases based off what was entered into the initial input. This is what I would consider to be an “Off-the-Shelf prototype which would allow me to gauge requirements for the UX/UI and gave me the ability to view an activity and conduct a feasible study. I used the “think out loud” method of testing here (and for many of my tests) in order to gauge the reaction of this primitive prototype. I also incorporated a user background questionnaire for four of my classmates and friends with the questions below.

Questions

1. On a scale of 1-10 how savvy would you say you are at navigating HTML websites?
2. On a scale of 1-10 how much knowledge do you have about web servers?
3. Have you ever sold anything online?
4. On a scale of 1-10 how entrepreneurial are you?
5. Have you ever used Etsy before

Results

1. Average: 8.5 Commentary: given that the people around me are college students, I guessed that this would be close to the average anyways
2. Average: 6 Commentary: It is not overly common for people outside of IST to have extensive knowledge of this subject, so I was not surprised by this either
3. Result: All said yes Commentary: I was surprised by this that all of the subjects had sold something online which gives me promise for the upcoming questions
4. Average: 7 Commentary: I would say this is a pretty solid score and not one that I was necessarily expecting
5. Result: All said yes Commentary: I was not surprised by this answer given how far of a reach the company has. This is partially why I chose this integration

What I gathered from this first rendition was the fact that the user design was not overly helpful in terms of recognizing functionality, and the results did not feel very useful. Despite many of the users never selling on Etsy, it seemed that without a fuller implementation, my test was not overly conclusive. However, the main takeaway from this part was the fact that I needed a better GUI user design and familiarity would have to play a major role.

Prototype Two (High Fidelity)

(Input)

(Output)

This second prototype was higher fidelity which allowed for me to better gauge the user experience. On the back end, this was a lot more fully functional with the ability to scrape one of the top listings (with the goal being three). However, to scrape one listing versus three actually turned out to be rather easy in the end. I still gave the same questionaries to the test group, but I carefully looked at the speak out loud reactions which were a lot more helpful this time. The major reactions and takeaways were as follows.

Key "Think-Out-Loud" Takeaways

User: RJ - the search bar is unclear as to what should be put in and the result is not overly clear as to where it came from
User: CJ - did not understand what was supposed to be put into the search bar (until I helped)
User: Julia - did not understand what was supposed to be put into the search bar (until I helped) and did not know what result was for
User: Patrick - was unable to access the system, uses Internet Explorer
User: Coleman - understood what to search for and got result

The conclusion that I made from this test was that I needed a more interactive demo of the product as well as a clear showing of where the data came from so that it could be trusted
